# Robot Kinematics and Dynamics

## Forward Kinematics

Forward kinematics (FK) computes the position and orientation of the end effector given the joint angles. For a serial robot with n joints:

T_end_effector = T_1(q_1) * T_2(q_2) * ... * T_n(q_n)

Where T_i(q_i) is the homogeneous transformation matrix for joint i as a function of joint angle q_i.

### Denavit-Hartenberg (DH) Convention
The DH convention parameterizes each link with four parameters:
- **d**: Offset along the previous z-axis to the common normal.
- **θ (theta)**: Angle about the previous z-axis (joint variable for revolute joints).
- **a**: Length of the common normal (link length).
- **α (alpha)**: Angle about the common normal.

### Product of Exponentials (PoE)
An alternative to DH, used in the Modern Robotics textbook by Lynch and Park. Each joint is described by a twist (screw axis), and the FK is a product of matrix exponentials:

T = e^{[S_1]θ_1} * e^{[S_2]θ_2} * ... * e^{[S_n]θ_n} * M

Where S_i is the screw axis of joint i, θ_i is the joint angle, and M is the home configuration.

Advantages of PoE over DH:
- No need for link frames — only the base frame and end-effector frame.
- Easier to derive and less error-prone.
- Naturally handles both revolute and prismatic joints.

## Inverse Kinematics

Inverse kinematics (IK) computes the joint angles that achieve a desired end-effector position and orientation. This is harder than FK because:
- The mapping is generally not one-to-one: multiple joint configurations may yield the same end-effector pose.
- Solutions may not exist if the target is outside the workspace.
- The problem is nonlinear.

### Analytical IK
- Derive closed-form solutions by exploiting the robot's geometry.
- Fast (microseconds) and exact.
- Only possible for specific robot geometries (e.g., 6-DOF arms with a spherical wrist).
- IKFast (from OpenRAVE) can automatically generate analytical IK solvers for many robots.

### Numerical IK
- Use iterative methods to solve IK:
  - **Newton-Raphson**: Linearize the FK at the current configuration and solve for the joint angle update.
  - **Jacobian pseudoinverse**: Δq = J†(q) * Δx, where J† is the pseudoinverse of the Jacobian.
  - **Damped least squares (Levenberg-Marquardt)**: Δq = J^T(JJ^T + λI)^{-1} * Δx. More numerically stable near singularities.

- Numerical IK is general (works for any robot) but slower and may not find all solutions.

### IK Solvers in ROS 2 / MoveIt 2
- **KDL**: Default in MoveIt 2. Numerical solver based on Newton-Raphson. Can be slow and may fail near singularities.
- **TRAC-IK**: Drop-in replacement for KDL. Uses both KDL-style iterative solving and sequential quadratic programming (SQP) in parallel. More robust — higher success rate.
- **IKFast**: Analytical solver generated by OpenRAVE. Very fast. Requires generating the solver for the specific robot.
- **BioIK**: Evolutionary optimization-based solver. Good for highly redundant robots or complex constraints.

## The Jacobian

The Jacobian matrix J(q) relates joint velocities to end-effector velocities:

ẋ = J(q) * q̇

Where ẋ is the 6D end-effector velocity (linear + angular) and q̇ is the vector of joint velocities.

### Uses of the Jacobian
- **Differential kinematics**: Compute end-effector velocity from joint velocities.
- **Inverse velocity kinematics**: Compute joint velocities for a desired end-effector velocity: q̇ = J†(q) * ẋ.
- **Singularity analysis**: A singularity occurs when the Jacobian loses rank (determinant approaches zero). At singularities, certain end-effector motions become impossible.
- **Force/torque mapping**: τ = J^T * F, where τ is the joint torques and F is the end-effector wrench.
- **Manipulability**: The manipulability ellipsoid (computed from J * J^T) indicates how easily the robot can move in different directions. The Yoshikawa manipulability index is sqrt(det(J * J^T)).

### Singularities
- **Boundary singularities**: Occur when the robot is fully extended or folded back. The end effector cannot move further in certain directions.
- **Internal singularities**: Occur when two or more joint axes align, reducing the effective degrees of freedom.
- Near singularities, small end-effector motions require very large joint velocities, which is dangerous.

## Redundancy

A robot is kinematically redundant if it has more DOF than needed to perform a task. For example, a 7-DOF arm performing a 6-DOF task (position + orientation) has 1 degree of redundancy.

### Advantages of Redundancy
- Can optimize a secondary objective while achieving the primary task (e.g., avoid joint limits, avoid obstacles, maximize manipulability).
- Can avoid singularities by using the extra DOF.
- Greater workspace and dexterity.

### Null-Space Motion
The null space of the Jacobian is the set of joint velocities that produce zero end-effector velocity. For a redundant robot:

q̇ = J† * ẋ + (I - J† * J) * q̇_0

The term (I - J† * J) * q̇_0 is the null-space component: it moves the joints without affecting the end effector. q̇_0 is an arbitrary joint velocity that drives the secondary objective.

## Dynamics

### Newton-Euler Formulation
Computes joint torques by propagating forces and torques from the end effector to the base (or vice versa). Efficient for inverse dynamics (computing torques given desired accelerations).

### Lagrangian Formulation
Derives the equations of motion from kinetic and potential energy:

M(q) * q̈ + C(q, q̇) * q̇ + g(q) = τ

Where:
- M(q): Mass/inertia matrix
- C(q, q̇): Coriolis and centrifugal terms
- g(q): Gravity vector
- τ: Joint torques

### Computed Torque Control
A model-based control law that uses the inverse dynamics model to linearize the robot dynamics:

τ = M(q) * (q̈_desired + Kd * ė + Kp * e) + C(q, q̇) * q̇ + g(q)

Where e = q_desired - q is the tracking error. This achieves decoupled, linear error dynamics.

## Workspace

### Types of Workspace
- **Reachable workspace**: The set of all points the end effector can reach in at least one orientation.
- **Dexterous workspace**: The set of all points the end effector can reach in all orientations. Always a subset of the reachable workspace.

### Workspace Analysis
- Depends on joint limits, link lengths, and joint types.
- Can be computed by discretizing the joint space and computing FK for each configuration.
- Important for robot selection and workcell design.

## Configuration Space

The configuration space (C-space) of a robot is the space of all possible joint configurations. For a robot with n revolute joints, the C-space is an n-dimensional torus (each joint wraps around from -π to π).

- **C-free**: The subset of C-space where the robot does not collide with obstacles.
- **C-obstacle**: The subset where collisions occur.
- Motion planning is the problem of finding a path through C-free from a start to a goal configuration.
- C-space is typically too high-dimensional to represent explicitly, which is why sampling-based planners (RRT, PRM) are used.
